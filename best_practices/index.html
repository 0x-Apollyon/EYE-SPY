Best Practices

Following these best practices greatly reduces your risk of falling victim to malware


Using a secure password manager

Most of a persons data is stolen from passwords stored in cleartext files or saving them in the inbuilt password managers webbrowsers have.
There are many password managers out in the market. I personally use KeepassXC and would like to suggest it to everyone reading this.


Preventing password re-use

Do not re-use passwords. If one gets leaked, a threat actor can access all linked accounts.
This includes common variations like adding @123, which provide little extra security and can be easily bypassed by hackers.


Using a good antivirus software

A good anti virus can often detect malware when you download them and prevent you from running it. There are many good AV softwares in the market. I personally use Kaspersky on many of my devices and even its free tier is very good.


Prevent running suspicious files downloaded from the internet

Dont download suspicious files you find on the internet. Even if you do download these files, you should scan them by using some malware analysis tools.
You can use a service like Virustotal or Malcore to scan the indiviual files to determine wether they are malicious.


Store confidential and private files after encryption

Private and confidential files such as company documents, government IDs and other files should be stored only after encrypting them with a secure encryption algortihm. You can use software such as Veracrypt and Proton Drive for this.
